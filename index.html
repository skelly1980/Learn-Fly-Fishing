Techniques

Casting

Peter Kutzer form the Orvis company explains how to make the basic cast with a fly rod. This is the foundation of how everyone who has ever casted a fly rod has learned to cast. Peter is an excellent teacher and this is in my opinion the simplest explanation of how to cast a fly rod that I have seen. Pay close attention to how he explains the rod needing to stop abruptly and the fly line straightening out behind you before making the forward cast. That is the most important part of learning to cast a fly rod. Making sure that the rod tip travels in a straight path is the next important part of this video. A straight path will produce a smooth and accurate cast. One thing that I would add is something that Peter touches on a bit but I would expand further on is using just the wrist when first learning to cast. Lock your elbow to your side and snap your wrist back hard and stop ot abruptly. After waiting about a second you can snap your wrist forward hard to get the cast to lay out in front of you.

Rigging

Something that isn't often explained to most people when beginning to fly fish is how to rig up your fly rod set up. When rigging up your fly rod there actually is a right way and wrong way to do it. Twisting and lining up the dots when putting the sections together is very important. Done incorrectly, the section will actually seperate on you when casting or worse they can break. I'm not a very tall person like Peter is so I put the bottom two section together, then the top two sections, and finally I put the whole rod together. Peter does mention a very important tip about putting a hat down on the ground to protect your reel when stringing the line up through the rod. Folding the flyline over when strining up your rod is an excellent tip and will save you a lot of headaches.

Flies

Choosing flies tends to be the most intimidating part of fly fishing to most beginners. They see how many different types of flies that are available and it can seem daunting to choose. This is actually not the case at all. Generally speaking twelve or so flies is about all you'll ever need to get yourself started. There are three types of flies dry flies, nymphs,and streamers. The first type is the only that actually requires a little bit of thought but the other two you can just pick one. There is no right or wrong when it comes to using a nymph or streamer. Dry flies can be simplified into two seperate catagories when it comes to how to choose one. The first and most important is the color. To simplify this even further all you need to do is observe the color of the color of the bugs or insects flying around the stream or river you are on and pick one that most closely matches there color. the next important element is the size and this is even simpler. Justt pick a size you believe to be closest and goahead and use it. If your not catching any fish on it got to a smaller sized fly. Don't waste your time thinking youneed to know the names of all the flies inorder to catch fish with them. You don't, you'll learn the names over time.

Information

This page will explain some very simple and easy ways to get yourself out fishing once you learn how to cast. I will make some recomendations on some ways to get out and have some fun while avoiding any type of frustrations you may encounter. When most people think about fly fishing they tend to envision a picturesque river somewhere in the middle of no where. This is not the best way to get started. Your local parks and town ponds have plenty of easy to catch fish for you to hone yor skills on before proceeding to a more technical style that most trout fishing requires.
